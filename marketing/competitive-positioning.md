# Competitive Positioning — American AI Solutions LLC
**Purpose:** Know exactly how to answer "Why you vs. X?" for every common competitor type. Use this in discovery calls, proposals, and LinkedIn content.

---

## THE COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE (Michigan, 2026)

### Who you actually compete against:

**1. Big 4 / National Consulting Firms** (Deloitte, Accenture, EY)
**2. Regional IT Consulting Firms** (local MSPs trying to add AI services)
**3. Freelance Developers** (Upwork, Toptal — often offshore)
**4. SaaS Product Companies** ("Buy our AI software" vs. custom build)
**5. The Client Doing It Themselves** ("We'll just use ChatGPT internally")
**6. "Wait and See"** — Not buying from anyone yet

Each requires a different response.

---

## VS. BIG CONSULTING FIRMS

**Their play:** High brand credibility. Large teams. Impressive decks.
**Their weakness:** They sell strategy, not builds. Junior staff do the work. Long timelines. $50K minimums.

**Your response:**
> "Big firms are great if you need a 200-page strategy report and a 2-year engagement. If you need something working in 4 weeks that shows up in your P&L — that's what we do. At Deloitte, a senior partner sells the deal, then a 25-year-old analyst in India builds it. With us, I'm the one on the call and I'm the one building it. Your project doesn't get handed off to someone who's never run a manufacturing floor."

**LinkedIn content angle:**
> *"Big consulting firms will charge you $150K to tell you what AI to buy. We charge $15K to build it. Both answers say 'predictive maintenance' — only one of them involves a running system on Week 4."*

**Use when:** Large manufacturer, healthcare system, or logistics company that's considering a major firm.

---

## VS. REGIONAL IT CONSULTANTS / MSPS

**Their play:** Existing relationships, IT credibility, "we can do AI too."
**Their weakness:** Their core business is infrastructure and helpdesk, not AI or workflow automation. They're repurposing devs who maintain networks. No AI depth, no ML experience.

**Your response:**
> "MSPs are great partners — I actually refer clients to them for infrastructure work. But AI automation is a different discipline. Your MSP knows how to keep your servers running. That's not the same as knowing how to architect a computer vision inspection system or build a dispatch optimization model. Ask them to show you three completed AI automation projects, not IT projects."

**Use when:** The client mentions their IT company suggested they wait, or their IT company "is also doing AI now."

---

## VS. FREELANCE DEVELOPERS (Upwork / Offshore)

**Their play:** Low cost. Fast proposal. Cheap.
**Their weakness:** No project management, no Michigan-specific context, no accountability, often disappear after delivery, no support.

**Your response:**
> "You can absolutely hire an offshore developer for $30/hour. Here's what I've seen happen: the project takes 4x longer than quoted because requirements weren't scoped properly, the final product needs constant changes, and when something breaks 3 months later there's no one to call. We charge more because we stay accountable. If the system doesn't perform to the metrics we agreed on, we fix it. A Upwork contractor has zero obligation to be there in 90 days."

**Price reality check:** A $5,000 Upwork project that takes 6 months and requires 3 rounds of revisions costs more than a $15,000 project that works in 4 weeks.

**Use when:** Cost-conscious client who mentions they're "just going to hire someone."

---

## VS. SAAS AI PRODUCTS

**Their play:** "Buy our AI software, it's turnkey, plug it in."
**Their weakness:** Built for generic use cases. Doesn't integrate with the client's existing systems without custom work. Monthly subscription forever. No competitive differentiation — every competitor can buy the same tool.

**Your response:**
> "SaaS tools are great for standard workflows. But your business isn't standard. [Specific client situation.] If a SaaS tool solved this out of the box, you would have bought it already. What we build is custom to your operation — it knows your data, your suppliers, your lanes, your specs. You own it. No monthly subscription, no data leaving your facility unless you choose it, and no one else in Michigan running the same 'AI' as you."

**The differentiation:** Generic AI = commodity. Custom AI = competitive moat.

**Use when:** Client asks "why wouldn't I just use [Salesforce Einstein / ServiceNow / Procore AI / industry-specific SaaS]?"

---

## VS. DOING IT THEMSELVES ("We'll just use ChatGPT")

**Their play:** "We can figure this out internally."
**Their weakness:** ChatGPT is a chat interface. Building production AI systems requires API integration, prompt engineering, error handling, data pipelines, and ongoing maintenance. Most businesses don't have these skills.

**Your response:**
> "ChatGPT is an amazing tool — I use it every day. But using ChatGPT and building an AI system are as different as using a calculator and building a financial reporting system. You can absolutely start with ChatGPT. The question is: do you have someone on staff who knows how to connect it to your TMS data, keep it from hallucinating on your quality specs, and maintain it when Anthropic updates their API? If yes — go for it, I'll answer questions. If not — that's what we build."

**Use when:** Tech-comfortable client who thinks they can DIY it.

---

## VS. "WAIT AND SEE" (No purchase decision)

**Their play:** "AI isn't ready yet" or "Let's wait until it's more proven."
**Their weakness:** This decision costs them every day. Their competitors aren't waiting.

**Your response:**
> "I respect the caution — a lot of AI hype is worth ignoring. But the automation we deploy isn't experimental. Automated dispatch matching has been running in trucking for 3 years. Computer vision quality inspection is in thousands of factories. Prior auth automation is in health systems right now. The question isn't 'is this ready' — it's 'how many more months do you want your competitor to have it while you don't?'"

**Michigan urgency angle:**
> "Your Tier 1 customer is already asking about AI readiness. This isn't a technology conversation anymore — it's a supplier qualification conversation. The shops that can say 'yes, we have AI-driven quality inspection' are getting on preferred supplier lists. The ones that say 'we're thinking about it' are getting off them."

**Use when:** Client seems interested but keeps deferring the decision.

## PRICING POSITIONING

**Never apologize for your price.** Reframe every time.

**When they say "that seems expensive":**
> "Let me put it in context. You said this problem is costing you [X hours/week / $Y/month]. At that rate, the project pays for itself in [X months]. After that, every month the system runs is pure margin improvement. What I build isn't a cost — it's a compounding return."

**When they ask for a lower price:**
> "I hear you on budget. Here's what I can do: we scope a Phase 1 that solves the highest-ROI piece first, at a lower entry point. You see the results, you decide if Phase 2 makes sense. That way you're not committing to the full project upfront — you're buying proof first."

**Anchor on what it costs to NOT fix it:**
> "Doing nothing isn't free. [Problem they described] is costing you [cost they mentioned] every month. This project costs [price]. You break even in [months]. After that, you're ahead every month permanently."
