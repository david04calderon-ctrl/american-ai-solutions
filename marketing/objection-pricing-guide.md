# Pricing Objection Handler
# American AI Solutions LLC

**Purpose:** "It's too expensive" is not an objection — it's a signal that you haven't connected value to the number. This guide gives you the exact language to handle every pricing conversation, reframe every budget concern, and close more deals without discounting.
**The rule:** Never drop your price without extracting something of value in return. Discounting without reason teaches the prospect that your price was arbitrary.

---

## THE PRICING FRAMEWORK (Understand This First)

Before handling objections, internalize why AAIS pricing is what it is:

**Quick Deploy ($7,500–$18,000):** 3–6 weeks of work by a skilled AI systems architect who also knows your vertical, Michigan's grant system, HIPAA/IATF compliance requirements, and how to document the system so your team can operate it. Compare this to:
- A single month of one IT employee ($5,000–$7,000)
- A year of Zapier + ChatGPT Team ($5,400)
- One trip to an IT consultant at $175/hour (43–103 hours of hourly billing for the same scope)
- One month of enterprise SaaS ($2,000–$5,000, forever, no customization)

**Full Implementation ($20,000–$45,000):** 8–14 weeks of architectural work connecting multiple systems, building custom models, training the team, and documenting everything. Net after grant: $8,000–$28,000 for a system that runs for years without additional cost.

**After the grant:** This is not a marketing line — it's math. Most Quick Deploy projects net $3,500–$8,000 after Going PRO reimbursement. Most Full Implementations net $8,000–$28,000. The prospect rarely does this math on their own.

## THE SIX PRICING OBJECTIONS (And Exactly How to Handle Each)

### OBJECTION 1: "It's too expensive."

**What they're really saying:** "I haven't seen the ROI yet."

**Wrong response:** Dropping the price immediately. This signals the number was arbitrary.

**Right response:**

> "I want to make sure I understand what 'too expensive' means in your situation. Is it the total number, or is it a cash flow timing issue? [Wait for answer.]"

If it's the total number:
> "Let me show you the math on this differently. [Pull up the specific pain point they mentioned in discovery.] You told me your team spends [X hours] per week on [process]. At [$Y per hour loaded rate], that's $[Z]/year in cost you're already paying for this problem. The system we'd build eliminates most of that. So the question isn't '$15,000 vs. $0' — it's '$15,000 one time vs. $[Z] per year forever.' Which one is expensive?"

If it's cash flow:
> "That I can work with. We do 50% at contract signing and 50% at delivery. The deposit is due when you sign — the second payment doesn't come until the system is live and you've seen it working. Would a 30/30/40 split help instead?"

---

### OBJECTION 2: "We need to get competitive quotes."

**What they're really saying:** "I'm not sure this is the right decision yet."

**Wrong response:** "Sure, let us know what you find." (They'll disappear.)

**Right response:**

> "That's completely fair — I'd do the same thing. Here's what I'd ask you to compare: when you get other quotes, ask them specifically what you're getting. Are you buying hours or a deliverable? Is the system yours at the end, or are you licensing it month to month? Do they know your vertical — [automotive/healthcare/logistics/construction]? Do they understand the Going PRO grant structure?

> Most quotes you'll get will be either hourly billing from a developer who's never worked in [their industry], or a SaaS subscription that doesn't connect to your existing systems. When you find a firm that does fixed-scope, vertical-specific, grant-structured implementations and transfers full ownership — I'd love to know what they quote."

---

### OBJECTION 3: "We don't have budget for this right now."

**What they're really saying:** Either the budget genuinely doesn't exist (rare), or the priority isn't high enough yet (common).

**Wrong response:** "When will you have budget?" (Kicks the can indefinitely.)

**Right response:**

> "Help me understand — is this a question of budget not existing, or budget not being allocated to this yet?"

If budget doesn't exist:
> "Two things worth knowing: first, the Going PRO grant is reimbursement — you pay us and the state reimburses your company. The net out-of-pocket is significantly lower than the sticker price. Second, I've structured a few projects as payment plans for the right client. If the numbers were different, would the project make sense?"

If budget isn't allocated:
> "What would it take to get this on next quarter's budget? I can help you build the internal case — the ROI math, the grant documentation, a one-page summary for whoever approves it. I've done this before for other clients who had the same conversation internally. Sometimes having a third party's analysis of the ROI makes it easier to move."

---

### OBJECTION 4: "Can you do it for less?"

**The ask-back before you answer:**

> "I want to understand what you're working with. What number would you need to see to make this move forward?"

[Listen to their number.]

If the gap is small (<15%):
> "I can work with that if we adjust scope slightly — specifically [X feature] would come out of this engagement. Everything else stays the same. Does that work?"

If the gap is large (>25%):
> "I'd be misrepresenting what I can deliver if I came down that far. Here's an alternative: we could do a scoped-down version — just [core workflow], not the full system. That comes in at $[lower number]. You get the most critical piece working, and we have a natural expansion path when budget opens up. Does that make more sense as a starting point?"

**Never discount without extracting something:**
Every price reduction should remove scope, not just reduce the number. Otherwise you're teaching the prospect that you're negotiating your own estimate.

### OBJECTION 5: "We could probably build this ourselves."

**Who says this:** Technical-leaning prospects (engineering managers, CTOs, plant IT staff), or founders who underestimate the vertical-specific complexity.

**Wrong response:** Defending your value without addressing their confidence.

**Right response:**

> "You might be right. What's the technical profile of the person who would build it — what's their background?"

[Listen.]

If they have genuine technical talent:
> "That's a real option. The question I'd ask is: can they do it in 6 weeks while keeping up with their current responsibilities? And does your IT team know the [OEM data format / HIPAA BAA requirements / FMCSA data structure] for this specific workflow? The system isn't the hard part — the vertical-specific requirements and the grant documentation are where most internal builds stall."

If their technical confidence is mostly the business owner's optimism:
> "I'd encourage you to get a read from your team on the actual effort involved. The typical internal estimate I hear is '3–4 weeks' — the typical internal reality I see when clients come back is '6–9 months, not finished.' Not because the team isn't capable — because they're running their day job at the same time. I'm not going to talk you out of trying it internally — but I'll still be here if the timeline slips."

---

### OBJECTION 6: "We tried AI before and it didn't work."

**This is the hardest one.** Someone sold them something that failed. Their skepticism is earned.

**Wrong response:** "Our approach is different." (Everyone says this.)

**Right response:**

> "Tell me what happened — what did you try, what were you hoping it would do, and what actually happened?"

[Listen carefully. Don't interrupt.]

Then:

> "What you're describing is [what they experienced — a chatbot that hallucinated / a tool that didn't connect to their system / a consultant who delivered a document but not a system]. That's a real failure, and it's more common than it should be.

> What I'd offer instead of asking you to take my word for it: let's do the $2,500 workflow audit first. In 48–72 hours, you get a documented map of exactly what we'd build and why, what data connects where, what the system actually looks like, and what you'd need to operate it. If after seeing that document you're not confident it would work — we stop there. You've invested $2,500 and you have a blueprint you own. If you are confident, we apply the $2,500 toward the implementation.

> The audit exists specifically for this situation — when trust has to be earned back."

## THE GRANT MATH PRESENTATION

Every pricing conversation should include this. Do the math live, on paper or shared screen.

**For a $15,000 Quick Deploy project:**

> "Before we talk about the number, let me show you the actual out-of-pocket. [Draw this out or show in a document]

> Project total: $15,000
> Going PRO Talent Fund reimbursement (AI training component): estimated $4,000–$8,000
> Industry 4.0 Tech Grant (50% of eligible costs, if manufacturing): up to $7,500
> Net cost after grants: $3,500–$11,000

> Now compare that to what you're currently spending on [the manual process]. You told me [X hours/week × $Y/hr = $Z/year]. At $3,500–$11,000 net, you break even in [Z calculation] months. Then the system runs indefinitely for near-zero additional cost.

> This is the math most clients haven't seen before they talk to me."

**Important:** Don't guarantee specific grant amounts — grant awards depend on application quality, timing, and available funds. Say "estimated" and "up to." The conservative estimates ($4,000 Going PRO, 50% Industry 4.0) are defensible.

## THE PAYMENT STRUCTURE OPTIONS

**Standard:**
- 50% at contract signing
- 50% at system delivery and go-live

**If cash flow is the objection:**
- 33% at contract signing
- 33% at midpoint (defined milestone)
- 34% at delivery

**If budget cycle is the issue:**
- Sign in current quarter (locks the engagement)
- First payment deferred to [first of next month / start of next quarter]
- Full payment due at delivery

**For larger engagements ($25,000+):**
- 25% at signing
- 25% at Phase 1 delivery
- 25% at Phase 2 delivery
- 25% at final delivery and handoff

**Never:**
- All payment at delivery (you carry all the risk)
- Monthly payment plans (creates accounts receivable complexity)
- Payment only on "success" (metrics are always contested)

## WHEN TO WALK AWAY

Not every prospect should become a client. Walk away when:

1. **They want to own your time, not buy a system:** "We just want someone to help us figure this out" without a specific deliverable → this becomes an hourly consulting engagement that never ends.

2. **Budget is genuinely not there:** A $2M revenue construction company with negative margins who needs AI "to turn things around" — the system can help, but if they can't pay the deposit, you can't help them.

3. **They're shopping for validation, not a vendor:** They've already decided to do nothing, they're just collecting quotes to tell their partner "we looked into it."

4. **The timeline is incompatible with the scope:** "We need this in 2 weeks" for a Full Implementation is a failure setup. Either rescope or decline.

5. **They want to own the system during build:** Clients who want to "be involved" in every technical decision typically extend timelines by 2–3× and introduce scope creep. Healthy involvement ≠ micromanagement.

**When you walk away, say this:**
> "Based on what you've described, I don't think this is the right fit right now — [specific reason, not vague]. I'd rather tell you that now than start a project that doesn't deliver what you need. If [circumstances change], reach out and we'll pick up where we left off."

This earns more referrals than a mismatched project ever would.
