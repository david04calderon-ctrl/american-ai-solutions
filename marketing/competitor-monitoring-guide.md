# Competitor Monitoring Guide
# American AI Solutions LLC

**Purpose:** You should never be surprised by a competitor in a sales call. This guide tells you exactly who the competition is by vertical, what they charge, where they're weak, and how to monitor them so you always have intelligence before the prospect does. Knowing your competition better than the prospect knows them is a competitive advantage that costs almost nothing.
**Frequency:** Review competitor intelligence monthly. Check Google alerts weekly.
**Rule:** Never name a competitor negatively in a sales call. Instead, reframe the comparison on your terms.

## THE COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE — BY TIER

### TIER 1: National Enterprise AI Firms (Rarely compete directly — but prospects mention them)

**McKinsey Digital / Boston Consulting Group Digital**
- What they do: AI strategy, digital transformation for Fortune 500
- Typical engagement: $500K–$5M+
- Timeline: 6–18 months
- Why they lose against AAIS: They don't build — they advise. They charge 10–50× more. They have no Michigan-specific knowledge. They send a 24-year-old analyst who's never seen a production floor.
- What prospects say: "We had someone from McKinsey come in" (usually to justify not moving forward)
- Counter: "McKinsey will give you a 200-page strategy deck. We'll give you a live system. Which one do you need?"

**Deloitte AI & Data / Accenture AI**
- Same profile as McKinsey — enterprise pricing, enterprise timelines, generic output
- Counter: "Those firms are built for companies with a $2M IT budget. We're built for Michigan manufacturers with a $15K–$40K budget who need results in 6 weeks, not 6 months."

**IBM Consulting**
- More implementation-focused than McKinsey, but still enterprise pricing
- Often pushed by IT departments who are familiar with IBM
- Counter: "IBM requires you to commit to their infrastructure. We build on what you already have — no lock-in."

### TIER 2: Regional MSPs Who Have Added "AI" to Their Website

This is the primary competitive threat — Michigan MSPs who see AI as a new service line and add it to their menu without deep expertise.

**Key Michigan MSPs to monitor:**

| Company | HQ | AI positioning | Weakness |
|---------|-----|----------------|---------|
| Logicalis | Detroit area | "AI strategy and implementation" | Generalist, no vertical specialization |
| OneITSM | Michigan | IT services + some AI | Primarily IT support, not AI builders |
| Sentinel Technologies | Michigan | Cybersecurity-first, added AI | Security focus — AI is secondary |
| Convergence Networks | Michigan | Managed services + cloud + AI | Generalist |
| NetLink | Michigan | IT + "AI-readiness assessments" | Assessment-focused, not builders |

**How to find out what MSPs are pitching:**
- Go to their website → "Services" or "AI" page
- Check their LinkedIn posts for the last 30 days
- Search LinkedIn for "[MSP name] AI" to see employee posts

**Where AAIS wins against MSPs:**
1. MSPs charge hourly ($125–$200/hr) — AAIS charges fixed scope. Clients hate hourly.
2. MSPs don't understand vertical-specific AI (prior auth, OEE dashboards) — AAIS is built for it
3. MSPs don't know the Going PRO grant structure — AAIS does
4. MSPs build on their preferred platforms (Microsoft, Cisco) — AAIS builds on what the client has
5. MSPs want 3-year managed services contracts — AAIS delivers and transfers ownership

**The AAIS counter when an MSP is in the conversation:**
> "MSPs are great at keeping your network running. That's infrastructure work. What we build is intelligence — a system that reasons about your data and gives you answers your IT team can't. Those are different problems and different skill sets."

### TIER 3: Freelance Developers Who Build Custom AI

**Where they come from:** Upwork, Toptal, LinkedIn freelancers, "AI developer" on Twitter/X

**Typical profile:**
- $75–$150/hour, 3–6 month timeline, hourly billing
- Strong Python/ML skills but no business ops experience
- No Michigan knowledge, no grant expertise
- No project management, no documentation, no training
- Disappears after delivery — no support

**What prospects say:** "I found someone on Upwork who said they could build this for $5,000."

**The counter:**
> "A developer who hasn't seen an automotive quality floor doesn't know the data formats, the OEM requirements, or the IATF compliance constraints your system needs to meet. That $5,000 quote will turn into $15,000 in scope creep and a system that needs rebuilding. We've scoped this exactly and that's what the fixed price covers — no surprises."

**The real differentiator from a freelancer:** AAIS brings the business intelligence of the vertical plus the technical execution. A Upwork developer brings technical execution only. For a commodity AI task (write me a chatbot), the freelancer might be fine. For an OEE dashboard that needs to handle Ford Q1 audit requirements, the vertical knowledge is the product.

### TIER 4: Vertical SaaS Platforms (Compete in specific verticals)

**Automotive:**
- **Sight Machine:** Production monitoring AI, $50K+ enterprise pricing, requires significant infrastructure
- **Tulip:** Factory floor apps, $25K+/year, requires tablet/device investment + change management
- **Plex Cloud ERP:** Includes dashboards, but costs more than an AAIS implementation in Year 1 alone
- **Counter:** "Those platforms require you to migrate your operation to their software. We build on top of what you already run — your PLCs, your existing ERP. Implementation takes 4–6 weeks, not 6–18 months."

**Healthcare:**
- **Suki AI:** $300+/provider/month for ambient clinical notes
- **Abridge:** Clinical documentation AI, newer, limited integrations
- **Cohere Health:** Prior auth AI — focused on payer side, not practice side
- **Counter for Suki/Abridge:** "At $300/provider/month, a 5-provider practice pays $18,000/year forever. Our implementation is $12,000 once, then $1,500/month if they want maintenance. They break even in 8 months and own the system."
- **Counter for Cohere:** "Cohere works with payers to streamline their side of prior auth. We automate the practice side — how you prepare and submit the request. Different problem."

**Logistics:**
- **Samsara AI:** Hardware + software ($200+/truck upfront, monthly subscription), strong dashcam AI
- **Motive (KeepTruckin):** Similar to Samsara, hardware-dependent
- **McLeod AI features:** Added to existing McLeod TMS, limited capabilities
- **Counter:** "Samsara is great for what it does — dashcam and telematics. We build the dispatch intelligence layer on top of whatever telematics you already have. No new hardware. We work with Samsara, Motive, or any ELD."

**Construction:**
- **Procore AI:** Adding AI features, but $700+/month baseline, built for large GCs
- **Buildertrend:** Residential focus, weak for commercial
- **PlanSwift / Bluebeam:** Takeoff tools, no AI — manual process
- **Counter:** "Procore at $700+/month is designed for a $50M GC with a full IT department. If you're running $3M–$20M in annual volume, we build the estimating AI on top of your existing takeoff software — Excel, PlanSwift, whatever you use — without the platform migration."

- Review pricing of vertical SaaS tools — they change pricing frequently
- Check if any national firms have opened Michigan offices or announced Michigan-specific focus

## THE COMPETITIVE INTELLIGENCE RULES

**Rule 1: Never mention a competitor by name in a sales conversation unless the prospect brings them up.**
Naming competitors puts their brand in the prospect's mind. Let them bring it up.

**Rule 2: When a prospect names a competitor, say "I know them" — then pivot to your differentiation.**
Never say "they're bad" or "they don't know what they're doing." It sounds insecure.

**Correct script:**
Prospect: "I've also been talking to [MSP competitor]."
David: "I know them — they do solid managed services work. What I'd ask you to compare is this: [MSP] charges hourly and manages your IT infrastructure. We build a specific AI system that automates [their pain point] on a fixed scope, and you own it after. Those are pretty different things. What does [their process] actually look like — have they shown you what the deliverable would be?"

**Rule 3: Position AAIS on your terms, not theirs.**
Don't defend against competitor strengths. Lead with what makes AAIS uniquely right for Michigan businesses. The grant angle, the vertical depth, the fixed scope, the ownership transfer — these are not standard.

**Rule 4: Know their price before the prospect does.**
Research every competitor's pricing. When a prospect says "[MSP] quoted us $X," you should already know if that's above or below market and what it likely includes.

**Rule 5: Update the competitive file after every sales call where a competitor is mentioned.**
Add what the prospect said, what they were offered, and how the conversation resolved. Over time, you'll have pattern intelligence about what competitors pitch and where they lose.
