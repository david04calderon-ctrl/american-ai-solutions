# Client Referral Program
# American AI Solutions LLC

**Purpose:** A referred lead converts at 3–5× the rate of a cold lead, arrives with trust already built, and costs $0 to acquire. Every satisfied client is a pipeline asset who doesn't know they're a pipeline asset yet. This system turns satisfied clients into active referral sources — without being transactional or desperate about it.
**The rule:** Ask for referrals at the moment of peak satisfaction, not at the end of a long relationship when the project is old news. Peak satisfaction = the 90-day results call, when the numbers are visible and the client just heard the ROI.

---

## WHEN TO ASK (THE TIMING IS EVERYTHING)

**Wrong time:** The day you deliver the system. The client is relieved but still absorbing whether it's going to work.

**Wrong time:** 6 months later. The excitement has faded. The system is part of their daily routine. They don't think about it anymore.

**Right time:** The 90-day check-in call. You've just shown them the numbers. The ROI is visible. They said something like "this has been really valuable." That sentence is the signal.

**Right after they express satisfaction, say:**

> "I'm really glad to hear that — that's exactly what this was designed to do. I want to ask you something, and feel free to say no: is there anyone in your network — another plant manager, a practice manager, a fleet operator — who you think is dealing with the same kind of problem? I'm not asking you to sell them anything — just an introduction. I'll take it from there."

Then stop talking. Let them think. Most people who've had a good experience will think of one or two names immediately.

## THE THREE REFERRAL PATHS

### Path 1 — The Direct Introduction

The client introduces David to a specific person by email or LinkedIn.

**Ask for this with:**
> "The easiest thing would be if you're willing to intro me by email. Something as simple as 'David, meet [Name] — I thought you two should connect.' I can draft something for you to forward if that's easier."

**Always offer to draft the intro email.** Most people want to make the referral but find the writing friction a reason to delay. Remove the friction.

**Draft intro email David provides to client:**

> Hi [Name] —
>
> I've been working with a Detroit-based AI firm called American AI Solutions LLC on [vague description of project — e.g., "automating our quality reporting process"]. It's been genuinely useful — we've [one result, e.g., "cut our weekly reporting time by about 15 hours"].
>
> I thought of you because [specific reason — e.g., "you mentioned struggling with the same kind of manual tracking issue at [event/when we talked]"]. Their founder David does a free 30-minute call — no pressure, just a conversation. His contact is david@americanaisolutionsllc.com.
>
> [Client Name]

**What David does immediately after the intro email:** Sends a direct email to the referred contact within 24 hours:

> Subject: [Client name] suggested I reach out
>
> [Name] —
>
> [Client name] forwarded me your contact — I appreciate the intro.
>
> I run an AI automation firm in Detroit focused specifically on Michigan [their vertical]. [Client name] said you might be dealing with [pain point they mentioned].
>
> I do a free 30-minute strategy call — no deck, no pitch, just a conversation about what's actually slowing you down and whether AI is the right answer. Here's my calendar: [Calendly link]
>
> David Calderon
> American AI Solutions LLC

---

### Path 2 — The Permission Referral

Client doesn't proactively introduce David, but gives permission to name-drop them in outreach.

> "Would it be okay if I reached out to some other [manufacturers / practices / carriers] in your area and mentioned that we worked together? I'd keep it vague — just say I've worked with another Michigan [their vertical] on a similar project."

Most clients say yes to this. It's lower commitment than a direct intro. David can then say in cold outreach:

> "I recently completed an AI implementation for another Michigan [automotive supplier / healthcare practice / fleet operator] similar to yours — I'm not at liberty to share their name, but I'd be happy to walk you through the kind of results we delivered."

This is honest, uses social proof without exposing the client, and raises the credibility of the outreach.

---

### Path 3 — LinkedIn Referral

Ask the client to post or engage on LinkedIn in a way that surfaces AAIS to their network.

**Lowest friction version:**
> "If you ever wanted to post something about the project on LinkedIn — even a brief mention of the results — I'd obviously appreciate it. I can draft something for you if that would be easier. No pressure at all."

**Medium version:**
> "Would you be willing to write a LinkedIn recommendation on my profile? It would mean a lot at this stage in building the business."

**What to send them as a draft (make it easy):**
> [Name] — here's a draft you can edit to your voice:
>
> "I worked with David Calderon at American AI Solutions LLC on an AI automation project for our [operation]. In [X] weeks, the system was live and we've seen [specific result]. What stood out was [communication / knowledge of our industry / speed of delivery]. If you're a Michigan [manufacturer / practice / carrier / contractor] thinking about AI, I'd start with a call to David."

## THE REFERRAL FEE STRUCTURE (FOR PARTNER REFERRALS — NOT CLIENT REFERRALS)

For partners (CPAs, bankers, MSPs, Michigan Works! contacts), a referral fee applies:
- 10% of the first project fee, paid within 14 days of receiving client payment
- Only on projects that close and are fully paid

For client referrals, do NOT offer a cash referral fee upfront — it can make the relationship feel transactional. Instead:

**For client referrals that close:** Send a thank-you gift ($75–$150 value — restaurant gift card, nice bottle, something personal to them) with a handwritten note. This is more memorable than a Venmo transfer and strengthens the relationship.

## THE REFERRAL TRACKING SYSTEM

In HubSpot, track every referral source:

1. Every new contact gets a "Source" property: Cold Email / LinkedIn / Inbound / Referral — [Name]
2. Every deal gets a "Referred by" property: [Contact name] or [Partner organization]
3. Build a HubSpot report: Deals by source → shows referral conversion rate vs. cold

At the end of each quarter, look at which referral sources produced closed deals. Send a personal note to the top referrers:

> "[Name] — I was reviewing Q[X] and I want to let you know that [X] of the clients I worked with came through an introduction from you. That means a lot to me and to the business. Thank you."

This surfaces who your best referral sources are — and keeps them active.

## THE LINKEDIN CONTENT REFERRAL FLYWHEEL

Every post David publishes on LinkedIn gets seen by his connections — which includes clients. When a client sees a post about [their vertical], two things happen:

1. They remember the project and the results
2. They forward the post to someone in their network ("thought of you")

This is a passive referral engine. Every piece of LinkedIn content is a referral prompt disguised as information.

**Tactics:**
- Tag a satisfied client in a relevant post (with permission): "Working with a team like [Company] on exactly this kind of challenge — the results speak for themselves."
- Share a result post about a project type (anonymized): "Just completed a quality dashboard for a Southeast Michigan Tier 2 supplier. Results: [numbers]. If this sounds like your operation, link in comments."

The client's network sees the post. Referrals happen without David asking.

## THE 90-DAY REFERRAL SCRIPT (FULL)

This is the complete conversation at the 90-day results call:

**After presenting the numbers:**

David: "These results — is this what you were hoping for when we started?"

Client: "Yes, honestly more than I expected."

David: "I'm really glad. We worked hard to get those numbers. [Pause.] I want to ask you something. I'm building this business in Michigan — it's the only market I focus on, and the way I grow is through people like you, who have seen it work, telling other people. Is there anyone in your network — another plant manager, another operator — who you think is dealing with the same kind of problem we just solved for you?"

[If they give names:]
"That's great. Would you be comfortable making an intro by email? I can draft something for you — would take you about 30 seconds to forward."

[If they hesitate:]
"Even if there's no one specific you're thinking of — would it be okay if I reached out to other Michigan [their vertical] operators and mentioned that I've worked with a company in your area on a similar project? I'd keep your name out of it unless you're comfortable with me using it."

[If they're enthusiastic:]
"I'll send you a draft intro email you can forward to [names they mentioned]. And I'll also send you a brief summary of the project results — something you can reference if someone asks what we built. That usually makes it easier."

The goal of this conversation: one name, one intro, one new pipeline entry. That's the metric.
