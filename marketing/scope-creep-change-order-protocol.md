# Scope Creep & Change Order Protocol
# American AI Solutions LLC

**Purpose:** Scope creep is the #1 reason consulting projects lose money. A client who starts with "build us a quality dashboard" and ends with "and also connect it to our customer portal and add mobile alerts and can you look at our scheduling system" isn't a bad client — they're engaged. But without a protocol, you deliver $40,000 of work for $15,000. This guide handles it before it ever becomes a problem.
**The rule:** Scope is a contract. Changes to scope are billable. Being clear about this upfront protects the client relationship — it doesn't threaten it.

---

## WHY SCOPE CREEP HAPPENS

Clients don't request scope creep maliciously. It happens because:
1. They discover more problems as you build — the dashboard reveals another gap
2. They told you about everything they needed, but not everything they *wanted*
3. A stakeholder not in the original discovery call has different expectations
4. The original scope wasn't specific enough to create a clear boundary

The solution isn't to say no more — it's to have a system that lets you say yes profitably.

---

## PREVENTION — THE SOW IS THE SHIELD

Every engagement starts with a Statement of Work that defines:

**What's included:**
- Specific deliverables (named, not vague)
- Number of systems integrated (e.g., "Connects to [ERP name] and [QMS name] — not all systems")
- Number of user training sessions (e.g., "Two 2-hour training sessions for up to 5 users")
- Revision rounds (e.g., "Two rounds of revisions to dashboard layout after initial delivery")
- Support period (e.g., "30 days of email/call support after go-live")

**What's explicitly excluded:**
This is the most important section most consultants skip.

> *Out of scope: Integration with systems not listed above, mobile application development, data migration from legacy systems, custom reporting beyond the defined dashboard, additional user training beyond two sessions, post-30-day support (available under separate retainer agreement).*

When a client asks for something out of scope, point to this section first. "That's not something we scoped for this project — let me show you what we can add and what it costs."

## THE CHANGE ORDER PROCESS

When a request falls outside the SOW, use this exact process. Never say "no" — say "yes, and here's what that costs."

### Step 1: Acknowledge the Request

> "That's a great idea — adding [X] would definitely improve the system. That's not in our current scope, but let me put together a quick change order so we can keep moving without delay."

Don't debate whether it's in scope. Don't apologize. Just move to the next step.

### Step 2: Define and Price the Change

Within 24 hours, send a Change Order document:

---

**CHANGE ORDER #[001]**
**Project:** [Client Name — Project Name]
**Date:** [Date]
**Submitted by:** David Calderon, American AI Solutions LLC

**Requested change:** [One sentence description of what the client asked for]

**Scope of change:**
- [Specific task 1]
- [Specific task 2]
- [Specific deliverable]

**Out of scope for this change order:** [Anything adjacent that you're NOT including — prevents the change order itself from expanding]

**Estimated effort:** [X] hours
**Change order price:** $[Y] (fixed)
**Timeline impact:** [X] additional days added to project delivery

**Terms:** 50% due upon approval of this change order. 50% due at delivery of the change scope.

**To approve:** Reply to this email with "Approved" or sign below.

---

### Step 3: Wait for Written Approval

Never start work on a change order until you have written approval. An email reply saying "Approved" is sufficient. Don't start because they said "sounds good" on a call.

**Why:** Verbal approvals are forgotten. Written approvals create accountability. If the relationship sours later, your paper trail shows every scope change was approved.

### Step 4: Log It

In HubSpot, add a note to the deal with:
- Change order number
- Date requested
- Date approved
- Amount
- Description

In Google Drive, add the approved change order to the client folder.

## CHANGE ORDER PRICING

Use this pricing guide for common changes:

| Change type | Pricing basis | Example |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| Add one additional system integration | $1,500–$3,500 | Add Samsara connection to existing fleet dashboard |
| Additional dashboard view or report | $800–$2,000 | Add a shift-level breakdown to existing OEE dashboard |
| Additional training session | $500–$1,000 | Third training session for a new team |
| Extended support period | $750–$1,500/month | Add 60 days to the 30-day support window |
| Add user roles/permissions | $500–$1,500 | Add a read-only view for the plant manager |
| Data export to new format | $500–$1,500 | Add CSV export that wasn't in original spec |
| Mobile-responsive view | $2,000–$5,000 | Add phone-friendly version of dashboard |
| New workflow (significant scope) | Full scoping + new SOW | "Can we also automate our RFI process?" |

**The minimum:** No change order below $500. Below that, absorb it as goodwill and note it in HubSpot. You're not tracking time — you're tracking whether the relationship is healthy.

## THE LANGUAGE THAT WORKS

When a client asks for something out of scope:

**What not to say:**
- "That's out of scope." (Sounds adversarial)
- "We can't do that." (Sounds limiting)
- "That would cost extra." (Sounds money-grubbing)

**What to say:**
> "I love that idea. That would make the system significantly more useful for [reason they care about]. That's not in our current SOW, so I want to handle it the right way — let me put together a quick change order so you can see the cost and timeline, and we can keep the project moving cleanly. Give me until tomorrow."

This language:
- Validates the request (makes the client feel heard)
- Explains why you're using a change order (not adversarial — it's clean process)
- Sets a 24-hour timeline (keeps momentum)

## MANAGING CLIENTS WHO PUSH BACK ON CHANGE ORDERS

Some clients will say "I thought that was included" or "that seems like it should be part of the project."

**Script:**

> "I completely understand — I want to make sure we're on the same page. In our SOW [reference specific page or section], the deliverable was [X]. What you're describing is [Y] — which would be a meaningful addition. I'm not trying to nickel and dime you; I just need to make sure I scope it accurately so I can deliver it well. Let me show you exactly what it would take."

If they push further:
> "I'll tell you what — if this is a small addition [under 2 hours], I'll absorb it as a courtesy. But if it's going to take meaningful development time, I need to document it properly. Let me size it up tonight and let you know which side of the line it falls on."

This gives you a graceful out for genuinely small requests while holding the line on significant scope expansion.

## THE "DISCOVERY SCOPE CREEP" — WHAT IT LOOKS LIKE

Scope creep that happens during a discovery call (before the proposal) is less obvious but equally dangerous. Signs:

- "And we'd also love if it could..."
- "While you're in there, can you also..."
- "Our [other department] might want to use this too..."

**During discovery, write down every "and also" — then explicitly exclude them from the proposal scope unless you're pricing for them.**

In the proposal, add an "Out of Scope" section that lists every "and also" you heard and explains it's available as a Phase 2 addition. This:
1. Prevents scope surprises after signing
2. Creates a natural expansion conversation (Phase 2 items from Day 1)
3. Shows the client you were listening

## WHEN A CLIENT REFUSES TO PAY FOR CHANGE ORDERS

This is rare when the process above is followed, but if it happens:

**Step 1:** Have a direct conversation.
> "I want to resolve this directly. The work we did on [X] was outside our original scope, documented in this SOW. I put together a change order for it [attach], which was approved [reference their approval email]. I need to receive payment to keep the project moving. What's your concern with the charge?"

**Step 2:** If they claim they didn't approve it:
Show them the approval email. This is why you always get written approval.

**Step 3:** If they still refuse:
Pause non-critical work on the project (don't threaten, just stop the out-of-scope work). Continue only the original SOW deliverables until resolved.

**Step 4:** If the relationship is at risk and the amount is small (<$2,000):
Consider absorbing it as a business decision, documenting it in HubSpot, and deciding not to work with this client again. A $1,500 dispute that kills a referral relationship is a net loss.

**Step 5:** For larger amounts:
Use Michigan Small Claims Court (under $6,500) or file a business dispute claim. Your signed SOW + change order + approval email is your evidence.
