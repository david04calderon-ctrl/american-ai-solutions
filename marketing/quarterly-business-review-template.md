# Quarterly Business Review (QBR) Template
# American AI Solutions LLC — Client Retention Tool

**Purpose:** Hold a 45-60 minute review with every active retainer client each quarter. Keeps the relationship strong, surfaces expansion opportunities, and prevents churn before it happens.
**When to start:** Quarter 2 of any engagement (Month 4+). First QBR = 90 days after project delivery.
**Tools needed:** HubSpot client record open, system dashboard screenshots pulled, ROI tracking spreadsheet

---

## WHY QBRs WIN CLIENTS FOR LIFE

Most consultants deliver, collect payment, and disappear. That's why clients churn.

A quarterly review proves three things:
1. You're still paying attention to their operation
2. The system is working (with data to prove it)
3. There's more value available if they want it

Clients who receive QBRs renew retainers at 3× the rate of clients who don't. Every QBR is also a warm, natural moment to discuss expansion — without being salesy.

## PRE-QBR PREP (Complete 48 hours before the call)

**Pull from the client's system:**
- [ ] Screenshot of the main dashboard — current vs. baseline
- [ ] Key metric report for the quarter (specific to their vertical — see below)
- [ ] Any error logs, exceptions, or issues that came up (and how they were resolved)
- [ ] HubSpot activity log — all touchpoints this quarter

**Prepare the QBR deck** (6 slides max — keep it tight):
1. Cover: Client name, Q[X] [Year] Quarterly Review, Date
2. Metrics: What the system did this quarter (vs. baseline)
3. Highlights: 2-3 wins — specific, with numbers
4. Issues resolved: Any problems encountered and how they were handled
5. Roadmap: What's next — recommended optimizations or expansions
6. Open discussion

**Key metric by vertical:**

*Automotive/Manufacturing:*
- Defects caught per 1,000 parts (vs. baseline)
- OEE percentage (vs. baseline)
- Time to generate quality report (vs. baseline)
- Downtime hours (vs. baseline)

*Healthcare:*
- Prior auth hours per week (vs. baseline)
- No-show rate (vs. baseline)
- Documentation time per visit (vs. baseline)
- Staff hours saved per month

*Logistics/Trucking:*
- Deadhead rate (vs. baseline)
- Dispatcher truck ratio (vs. baseline)
- Driver retention rate (vs. baseline)
- Revenue per truck per week (vs. baseline)

*Construction:*
- Estimating hours per bid (vs. baseline)
- Change order capture rate (vs. baseline)
- PM reporting time per week (vs. baseline)
- Bids submitted per month (vs. baseline)

## QBR CALL STRUCTURE (45–60 minutes)

### 0:00–5:00 — Open + Agenda

> "Good [morning/afternoon], [Name] — appreciate you making time. I've got about 45 minutes of material here. I'll start with what the system did this quarter, show you the numbers, we'll talk through anything that's on your mind, and then I want to share a few things I think are worth discussing for the next 90 days. Does that work?"

Quick personal check-in — one question about their business, not about your system. What's changed, what's new, what's been hard this quarter for them as a business.

---

### 5:00–20:00 — Metrics Review

Walk through your 6-slide deck. For each metric:

**Script:**
> "At the start of the engagement, your [metric] was [baseline]. This quarter, it's running at [current]. That's a [X%] improvement. In dollar terms, that's roughly [$X] in [saved time / reduced waste / recovered revenue]."

Be specific. Don't say "things are running well." Say "your defect catch rate went from 82% to 96% — that's 14 fewer escapes per 1,000 parts, and at your volume that eliminates roughly 3 warranty claims per quarter."

**If a metric is down or flat:**
> "I want to be straight with you on [metric] — we're not where I want to be. Here's what happened: [explanation]. Here's what I've already done / what we're doing next: [action]."

Never hide a bad number. Clients who trust you through a bad quarter stay for years.

---

### 20:00–30:00 — Open Discussion

> "Before I go into what I'm thinking for the next 90 days — what's on your mind? What's been frustrating, what's working better than you expected, anything I should know about?"

**Listen for:**
- New pain points that haven't come up before
- Changes in their operation (new equipment, new contracts, headcount changes)
- Friction points with the current system
- Things they're doing manually that feel like they shouldn't be

**Take notes.** These are expansion opportunities.

Common expansion signals and how to flag them:
- "We're adding a second shift" → scaling the system
- "We got a new contract with [OEM]" → new data requirements, audit readiness
- "We're struggling with [thing not currently automated]" → new workflow
- "I've been trying to figure out [X]" → AI opportunity

---

### 30:00–40:00 — Next Quarter Roadmap

Present 2-3 specific recommendations. Not a sales pitch — a professional opinion on what would move the needle.

**Script:**
> "Based on what I'm seeing in the data and what you just told me — here's what I think is worth tackling in the next 90 days."

**Format for each recommendation:**
1. What it is (one sentence)
2. Why it matters for their operation (one sentence)
3. What it would take (rough scope + timeline)
4. Option A: within retainer / Option B: small add-on project

Example:
> "The machine downtime alerting is working well for Line 2. Extending it to Lines 3 and 4 is a 2-week project — we'd add 8 more sensors and hook them into the same dashboard. I can scope that as a $3,500 add-on, or we can fold it into the retainer for next quarter at a slightly higher rate. Worth it given you mentioned the Line 3 issue last month."

---

### 40:00–50:00 — Retainer Renewal / Expansion Discussion

If the retainer is up for renewal, address it directly. Don't leave it in email.

> "[Name] — your current retainer runs through [date]. Based on this quarter's results and what we've talked about today, I'd like to continue the work. Here's what I'm recommending for Q[X+1]..."

**Option A: Same retainer**
> "Continue at the same level — I'll keep the system running, handle optimizations as they come up, and we'll have another call like this in 90 days."

**Option B: Expanded retainer**
> "I'd recommend bumping to the [next tier] retainer to accommodate [specific expansions discussed]. That's $[X]/month vs. your current $[X]/month. Here's specifically what changes."

**Option C: New project + retainer**
> "There's a project here worth doing as a standalone, and I'd like to stay on retainer to support it through delivery and beyond. Let me send you a scope within 48 hours."

---

### 50:00–60:00 — Close + Action Items

Recap the call in 3-4 bullet points.

> "Quick recap of what we talked about:
> - [Metric] improved [X%] this quarter
> - [Issue] has been resolved — [how]
> - We're recommending [specific next step]
> - I'll send you [deliverable] by [date]
>
> Before we go — is there anyone in your network, a peer at another company, who's dealing with a similar challenge? I build the business entirely on referrals and word-of-mouth. If there's someone you'd feel comfortable introducing me to, I'd really appreciate it."

## CLIENT HEALTH SCORING (Do After Every QBR)

Rate each client 1–5 across 5 dimensions. Total score = client health score.

| Dimension | 1 (Risk) | 3 (Stable) | 5 (Thriving) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Results** | Metrics flat or worse | Modest improvement | Strong, visible improvement |
| **Engagement** | Unresponsive, disengaged | Replies within 48hr | Proactively shares updates |
| **Adoption** | Team not using the system | Partial use | Full team daily use |
| **Expansion** | Shrinking scope | Stable retainer | Actively expanding |
| **Advocacy** | No referrals, no reviews | Neutral | Sending referrals, wrote review |

**Score interpretation:**
- **20–25:** Champion client. Ask for referrals at every touchpoint. Consider a case study.
- **14–19:** Stable. Continue current approach. Identify one dimension to improve.
- **10–13:** At risk. Schedule a call within 2 weeks. Find out what's wrong.
- **Below 10:** Churn likely. Immediate conversation. Offer a reset call. Have a plan.

## THE CONVERSATION WHEN A CLIENT WANTS TO CANCEL

This happens. Be ready.

**The moment they say "we're thinking about pausing the retainer":**

> "I appreciate you telling me directly. Help me understand — is it about the results, the value you're getting, or something else?"

**Listen first. Then:**

If it's results → "Let's fix that. What specifically isn't working? I'd rather rebuild something than lose you."

If it's budget → "What's the budget you're working with? There may be a version of the retainer that fits. Or I can do a project-based engagement instead of monthly — that gives you control over the spend."

If it's "we can handle it ourselves now" → "That's actually the best outcome. You've got the system, you've got the SOPs. The retainer going forward is just insurance and optimization. If you want to pause and come back in 90 days, I can hold your rate."

If it's truly over → "Understood. I want to end this well. Let me make sure your team has everything they need — updated runbook, all credentials documented, and I'm available for questions for 30 days after our last invoice. That's part of what you paid for."

Never argue. Never guilt-trip. A graceful exit builds referrals.

## QBR CADENCE BY CLIENT TIER

| Client Tier | Retainer Level | QBR Cadence | Format |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 (Anchor clients) | $2,500+/month | Quarterly + monthly 15-min check-in | Full deck + video call |
| Tier 2 (Core clients) | $1,200–$2,500/month | Quarterly | Slides + video call |
| Tier 3 (Entry clients) | Under $1,200/month | Biannual | Summary email + 30-min call |
| Project-only clients | No retainer | 90-day post-delivery check-in | 20-min call |

**Goal:** Convert every project-only client to a retainer by Month 2. Use the 90-day check-in as the conversion moment — "The system's running well. Here's what I'd want to monitor going forward..."
