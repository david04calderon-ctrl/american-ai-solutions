## The Deadhead Problem Is Bigger Than You Think

Deadhead mileage — miles driven empty, not generating revenue — is the single largest controllable cost in most Michigan fleets. Industry average: 20–28% of total miles driven are empty.

Here's what that costs a typical Michigan operation:

**Fleet: 50 trucks, 150,000 miles/year per truck**
- Total miles: 7,500,000
- Deadhead at 22%: 1,650,000 empty miles
- Cost per mile (fuel + driver + depreciation): $1.20
- Annual deadhead cost: **$1,980,000**

Reduce deadhead by 5 percentage points — from 22% to 17% — and you recover:
- 375,000 fewer empty miles
- **$450,000/year in recovered revenue and avoided cost**

That's not a rounding error. That's a dispatcher's annual salary. That's a down payment on three new trucks. That's the difference between a carrier that margins at 8% and one that margins at 12%.

AI dispatch optimization doesn't eliminate deadhead — nothing does. But consistently reducing it by 4–7 points is achievable in the first 90 days of deployment.

## What Dispatcher Ratio Actually Means

The trucking industry measures dispatcher efficiency as a ratio: trucks per dispatcher. National average for a well-run carrier: 30–35 trucks per dispatcher.

What happens when AI handles the routine load matching, HOS calculation, and lane assignment?

Dispatcher ratio moves to 60–80 trucks per dispatcher.

Not because dispatchers work harder. Because they stop doing the work AI can do faster — and focus on the judgment calls, the customer relationships, and the exceptions that still need a human brain.

**What that means for hiring:**

A carrier that grows from 50 to 100 trucks without AI needs to hire 2–3 dispatchers. With AI dispatch optimization, that same carrier may add 1 — or none, depending on how the AI layer is configured.

At $55,000–$70,000 per dispatcher (salary, benefits, training), avoiding two hires is $110,000–$140,000/year in recurring cost. The AI system that enables it costs $12,000–$20,000 once.

## What AI Dispatch Actually Does (And Doesn't Do)

**What it does:**

- Scans the load board, your TMS, and broker portals simultaneously — matches backhaul loads to drivers within minutes of delivery, not hours
- Monitors HOS logs in real time — flags drivers approaching limits before a violation, not after
- Calculates deadhead cost per proposed load against current fuel prices and lane averages — surfaces loads that look good but don't pencil
- Tracks lane-by-lane profitability — identifies which lanes are generating margin and which are eroding it
- Sends driver assignments directly to driver phones via existing ELD app (Samsara, Motive, or your current provider)

**What it doesn't do:**

- Replace the dispatcher — complex situations, customer escalations, and driver relationship management still require a human
- Require new hardware — it builds on top of your existing TMS (McLeod, Aljex, TMW, or even Excel-based operations)
- Fix broken rate structures — if your contract rates are underwater, the AI tells you that clearly, but you still negotiate the new rates
- Guarantee load availability — it optimizes what's available, not what doesn't exist

The distinction matters because some AI vendors oversell automation. The systems that actually work are the ones that make dispatchers 2–3× more effective, not the ones that claim to eliminate them.

## The Michigan Corridor Advantage

Michigan's geographic position creates a specific dispatch optimization opportunity most national AI vendors don't understand.

The I-94/I-75/I-69 triangle — Detroit to Chicago, Detroit to Cleveland, Detroit to Grand Rapids — is one of the densest freight corridors in the Midwest. Michigan carriers running automotive freight (Tier 1/2/3 plant-to-plant), LTL consolidation through Detroit, and agricultural hauls out of West Michigan operate in a corridor where backhaul matching density is unusually high.

What this means practically: the AI load-matching algorithm can be trained on Michigan-specific lane data — which loads are available at what times, which brokers post reliably, which lanes have consistent rate floors. A generic national dispatch AI uses national averages. A Michigan-calibrated system uses Michigan reality.

This is one of the reasons Michigan-specific AI implementation outperforms off-the-shelf national platforms for Michigan carriers.

## The Integration Question: Do You Need to Replace Your TMS?

No. This is the question every fleet manager asks, and the answer is always no.

AI dispatch optimization works as a layer on top of your existing TMS — not a replacement for it. The AI reads data from your TMS (driver locations, active loads, HOS status), processes it, and sends recommendations back — either as a dashboard your dispatcher uses, or as direct suggestions pushed to the TMS interface your team already knows.

Integrations tested and verified:
- McLeod Software
- Aljex (now Samsara TMS)
- Motive (KeepTruckin) ELD + TMS
- Samsara Fleet Management
- Excel-based dispatch (yes — even this works)

The migration risk is near-zero. The learning curve for dispatchers is measured in hours, not weeks. The system starts producing data on day one.
